What native plants actually do

Native species are not decorative. They are the infrastructure that makes the land work.

  • Pollination: Without native-supported pollinators, MSU says there would be little to no fruit, fewer vegetables, and many plant species would not survive.
  • Stormwater: Native prairie absorbs 30% more rainfall than lawn, reducing flooding downstream.
  • Carbon: Deep-rooted natives store more carbon than turfgrass or young invasive shrubs.
  • Air quality: Native trees remove particulates linked to asthma and heart disease.
  • Temperature: Canopy at 40% coverage drops measured street temperatures 7-9 degrees F.
  • Food webs: Native plants support the insects that support the birds. Remove the base layer and the whole chain above it weakens.

Michigan's own DNR Wildlife Action Plan already frames wildlife populations as in decline and uses uncommon species as indicators of land, water, and air health.

Doing nothing is not a neutral choice. On disturbed land, leaving invasives in place means the disturbance keeps compounding. Ecological neglect accrues interest.

The removal trap Removal without replanting is incomplete. Bare disturbed soil after invasive treatment will get re-invaded unless native or otherwise desirable plants are established right away. (KISMA / Michigan Tech native landscaping guide)

What's on Detroit's vacant land right now

Detroit has roughly 122,929 vacant lots - 19 square miles. Most of that land is already occupied by invasives:

  • Remove Common and glossy buckthorn, autumn olive, Amur honeysuckle, multiflora rose, Oriental bittersweet
  • Remove Ailanthus (Tree of Heaven) - hosts spotted lanternfly, which is now in 6 Michigan counties
  • Manage Non-native Phragmites, reed canary grass, purple loosestrife
  • Keep Native Phragmites (reddish stems, subsp. americanus) - do not remove

Each vacant lot carries roughly 735,000 viable invasive seeds, each capable of spreading into surrounding properties, parks, and waterways.

Common objections - and what to say back

Myth
"Plants are plants. Green is green."
Reality
Michigan defines invasives as non-native species that cause harm by outcompeting natives and changing ecosystem balance. Fast growth is often proof of ecological imbalance, not ecological fit.
Myth
"Nature will sort it out."
Reality
In Michigan, habitat destruction is the biggest pressure and invasives are ranked #2. "Leave it alone" on damaged land usually just means letting the second-biggest threat intensify inside land already weakened by the first.
Myth
"Removal is just aesthetic."
Reality
Native species support pollination, food webs, water filtration, and flood control. This is ecosystem function, not landscaping preference.
Myth
"We can deal with it later."
Reality
Later means larger infestations, weaker native seed banks, more disturbed soil, higher treatment cost, and a longer restoration cycle. The emerald ash borer cost Michigan $38M+ after it established. Spotted lanternfly is on the same trajectory right now.
Myth
"This is too small to matter."
Reality
Small untreated patches are seed sources. Michigan lists hitchhiking, firewood transport, and road corridors as major spread pathways. Every unmanaged lot is a launch point for the next block.

The evidence that rehabilitation works

This is not theoretical for Detroit. A 2025 MSU study using acoustic monitoring at 110 sites across 11 Detroit neighborhoods found 54 bird species on vacant land. Four rehabilitated parks that received invasive removal and native planting showed measurable increases in bird and plant diversity after treatment.

The land does not get healthier by being left alone. Site conditions change when restoration changes them.

The local argument in one sentence In Detroit, the question is not whether biodiversity matters. The question is whether 19 square miles of vacant land will be managed as living infrastructure or left to degrade into repeated ecological and financial liability.

What you can do on your block

  • Remove buckthorn, autumn olive, and honeysuckle before they fruit (fall timing matters)
  • Replant immediately after removal - bare soil re-invades fast
  • Use MNFI's 3-tier priority list to decide what to tackle first
  • Add even one native prairie or edge plant per 100 sq ft - it compounds
  • Log species observations on iNaturalist - data helps track what's working
  • Detroit Land Bank sells adjacent side lots for $100 - it is acutally accessible
