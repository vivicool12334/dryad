The Problem

Detroit has over 100,000 vacant lots: 18 square miles of unmanaged land running through neighborhoods already carrying the city's highest heat, asthma, and life-expectancy burdens. The city spends $6.7 million a year just to mow them. Not to restore them. Not to steward them. Just to keep neglect from taking over completely.

The Harm Is Already Here

These lots are not empty. They trap heat, harbor invasive growth, and sit where canopy is scarcest. 107 deaths are projected in a single severe heatwave. Detroit children have the worst asthma rate in the nation at 28.6%, and life expectancy swings 13–22 years between neighborhoods. The clinical fix is trees and green space, but the hardest-hit areas have the least.

Planting Isn't Enough

We know how to start restoration. We keep failing at the part after planting. 50% of urban trees are dead within 15 years, with 6.6–7% annual mortality. More than 80% of lifetime cost comes after planting, in the maintenance most grants never fund. When the grant ends, the site reverts. The ribbon-cutting survives. The habitat does not.

Neglect Favors Invasives

Unmanaged lots do not stay neutral while we wait. Each one can hold up to 735,000 viable invasive seeds. Detroit has already lost 175,000+ trees since 2012 to Emerald Ash Borer alone, and spotted lanternfly is now endemic in Wayne County, threatening Michigan's $90B+ agriculture industry. Left alone, vacant land becomes a distribution system for ecological loss.

The core failure is not intention. It is continuity. Restoration has no persistent operator: no entity that monitors the land every day, pays contractors on time, verifies the work, and is still there after the grant report is filed.

Ecological Collapse

While the city pays the fiscal cost, the land itself is deteriorating. Native trees and plants are not decorative. They clean air, filter water, cool streets, hold soil, and feed the pollinators behind one in three bites of food. Every native species supports hundreds of organisms in a chain thousands of years old. When invasives replace them, that chain breaks and the services disappear.

This is already happening. Unmanaged vacant lots have become launchpads for invasive species that outcompete native plants, strip food sources from pollinators, and spread into surrounding neighborhoods, farms, and waterways. Not a future risk. A collapse in progress.

Why native ecosystems matter to you personally: Native trees remove the air pollution that causes asthma and heart disease. Native root systems prevent flooding by absorbing stormwater that invasive monocultures cannot handle. Native plants feed the pollinators behind one in three bites of food you eat. When a local ecosystem collapses, the city replaces those services with engineered alternatives (stormwater drains, air filtration, flood walls) that cost orders of magnitude more and work worse.

The cascade effect: Emerald ash borer killed over a million ash trees in SE Michigan. With the canopy gone, sunlight hit the forest floor and invasive shrubs like buckthorn took over. Buckthorn shades out native wildflowers. Without wildflowers, pollinators starve. Without pollinators, native trees can't reproduce. One invasive insect triggered a chain reaction that is still degrading ecosystems 20 years later. The same pattern is beginning with spotted lanternfly and Tree of Heaven on vacant lots right now.
Early detection is 100× cheaper than late response. The emerald ash borer cost Michigan $38M+ after it established, all spent on removal, not prevention. Spotted lanternfly is following the same trajectory, and it breeds on Tree of Heaven, which has colonized every vacant lot in Detroit. Dryad's iNaturalist-powered monitoring catches new invasions early, when removal costs dollars instead of millions.

Sources: MDARD SLF Status (2025), Penn State Extension cost model (2023), USDA APHIS EAB assessment, Raupp et al. Annual Review of Entomology (2006), Tallamy, Nature's Best Hope (2019)
