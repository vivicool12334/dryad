Hi Sarah,

My name is Nick George — I built a project called "Dryad" and I'm wondering if you might be interested in working together on bringing it to life in some capacity.

It's a theoretical AI agent framework for autonomous land management. The idea is pretty simple: what if a piece of land could manage itself? An AI agent runs a 24-hour loop, checks weather, monitors species through iNaturalist, coordinates contractors for invasive removal, manages its own treasury through DeFi yield, and records everything onchain. The whole thing is designed to be self-sustaining after a one-time setup investment, running at about $105/lot/year, less than what the city pays to mow.

I haven't purchased the land yet. The system is built and functional, but I'm currently figuring out the right fundraising strategy and exploring grant opportunities before acquiring lots through the Land Bank. I'm also looking for a nonprofit to act as my fiscal sponsor for grant applications. The target is 9 lots on 25th Street in Chadsey-Condon, restoring lakeplain oak openings and wet prairie, the globally imperiled communities that used to cover this whole region.

The reason I'm reaching out to you specifically is your "Saving for the Future" report. You're describing exactly the problem I've been trying to solve — long-term stewardship at scale. Circle Forest is beautiful, but getting from 1.3 acres to 18 square miles of vacant land needs some kind of operational layer that doesn't depend on volunteer labor forever. That's what Dryad is.

I'm not trying to compete with anything DFC is building. If anything, I think Dryad could be a useful tool for the GreenSpace Conservancy, the technology infrastructure underneath the institutional framework you're standing up. Maybe a set of lots could be an early pilot site, or maybe there's a completely different way to collaborate. I'm open.
