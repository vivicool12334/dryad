## Project Summary

I built a project called Dryad — it's a theoretical AI agent framework for autonomous land management on vacant lots. The core idea is that a piece of land should be able to take care of itself.

The target is 9 vacant lots on 25th Street in Detroit's Chadsey-Condon neighborhood, available through the DLBA's Create-a-Project program at $250/lot. I haven't purchased the land yet. I'm currently exploring grant opportunities and figuring out the right fundraising strategy, and I'm in the process of finding a nonprofit to act as my fiscal sponsor for grant applications. The system is built and functional, but I want to make sure the funding and institutional support are in place first. Detroit has over 100,000 vacant lots across 18 square miles, and the city spends $6.72 million a year just mowing them. These lots are a fiscal drain and an ecological blank canvas at the same time.

Dryad is an AI agent that runs a 24-hour autonomous loop: it checks the weather, monitors biodiversity through iNaturalist community science, coordinates contractors for invasive species removal, manages a self-sustaining treasury funded by DeFi staking yield, and records ecological milestones onchain so anyone can verify the impact. The target ecosystem is lakeplain oak openings and wet prairie, globally imperiled plant communities (less than 1% of historical range survives) that once covered this region.

The financial model is designed to be permanently self-sustaining. At target treasury ($27,000 in staked ETH at ~3.5% annual yield), ongoing operations cost about $105/lot/year, less than the city's mowing cost, while delivering stormwater management, carbon sequestration, pollinator habitat, and urban heat reduction. After the initial establishment investment, it shouldn't need another grant.

All code is open-source and designed to be replicated on any city's vacant land.

## Needs Statement

The problem isn't that nobody cares about Detroit's vacant lots. It's that long-term stewardship doesn't scale. Community gardens and volunteer greening projects do incredible work on individual lots, but they depend on sustained human commitment over years. Native prairies need 2-3 years of active establishment, regular mowing to suppress invasives, before they become low-maintenance. Most projects can't maintain that.

Dryad takes the repetitive operational loop off people's plates. The agent handles weather monitoring, species tracking, contractor coordination, and financial management. Community members still do the ground-truthing, submitting observations through iNaturalist, uploading photos through the submission portal. The agent operates within strict guardrails ($50/transaction limit, $200/day, allowlisted addresses only).

Chadsey-Condon has among the highest vacancy rates in Detroit. The Philadelphia Vacant Land study found a 63% reduction in poor mental health, 37% reduction in crime perception, and 5% property value increase near greened lots. There's real evidence that this kind of work changes neighborhoods.

## Expected Outcomes

**Year 1:** 9 lots cleared of woody invasives. Native seed mix installed. 300 saplings planted. 30+ species documented on iNaturalist. Autonomous loop operational.

**Year 2:** Prairie establishing (grasses 1-3 ft, first forb blooms). 2-3 mowing cycles to suppress regrowth. 50+ species documented. Treasury generating yield.

**Year 3+:** Self-sustaining. Prairie grasses dominant. Oak saplings 6-8 ft. No further funding needed.

**Years 5-10:** Mature prairie (50-100+ species). Oak savanna structure. Deep root systems providing stormwater management and carbon sequestration. Potential habitat for state-threatened Kirtland's snake and federally endangered rusty-patched bumble bee.

## Why This Doesn't Need Perpetual Funding

This is the part I think is most interesting for a foundation with a spend-down timeline. Dryad is designed as a one-time investment that creates permanent, self-sustaining ecological infrastructure.

The treasury has adaptive spending modes — NORMAL, CONSERVATION, and CRITICAL — so even in a 50% ETH price decline, the non-negotiable floor ($383/year for taxes, hosting, gas, and LLC) stays covered. The prairie itself gets cheaper over time as natives outcompete invasives. Year 1-2 costs $1,445/year. Year 3+ drops to $945. The land does most of the work once it's established.

You invest once. The forest runs forever.
