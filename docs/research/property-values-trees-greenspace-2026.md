# Property Values, Mature Trees, and Green Space

> Research brief for Dryad. Compiled May 2026.
> Purpose: turn the property-value uplift from urban trees and greened lots into a defensible selling point for residents, neighbors, donors, grantors, and city partners.

---

## TL;DR (use this in pitches)

1. **Blighted vacant lots drag down adjacent property values by roughly 4 to 20 percent.** That drag flips to a premium of 5 to 30 percent once those lots are cleaned, planted, and maintained.
2. **Mature street trees and a tree-canopied block are worth real money.** A single street tree raises the sale price of the home it fronts by about $7,100 and lifts nearby homes within 100 feet by an additional $5,700 ($12,800 total uplift per tree, Portland 2010 dollars).
3. **Neighborhood canopy compounds.** A 10 percent increase in canopy near a home is associated with a 1 to 2 percent increase in sale price; well-treed blocks command 3 to 15 percent premiums citywide.
4. **The biggest wins land in the lowest-value neighborhoods.** The Philadelphia LandCare program raised property values within 1,000 feet of greened lots by 4.3 percent in year one and 13 percent cumulatively over six years. Community gardens lift surrounding values by up to 9.4 percent in the poorest neighborhoods.
5. **The benefits stack.** Trees also cut energy bills 20 to 50 percent, capture stormwater, reduce violent crime by ~9 percent on the block (Branas RCT), and lower asthma and heat-related illness. Every $1 spent on urban trees returns $2 to $5.

For 9 lots in Chadsey-Condon, that arithmetic isn't abstract. It is the explicit case for why every neighbor should want Dryad to succeed on the lot next to theirs.

## What the strongest studies actually say

### Donovan & Butry (2010): the canonical Portland study

USFS Pacific Northwest research forester Geoffrey Donovan and David Butry of NIST used a hedonic regression on Portland single-family home sales, controlling for house and neighborhood characteristics. They isolated two effects:

- The number of street trees directly fronting a house.
- The canopy cover within 100 feet of the home.

Both raised sale price independently. The midpoint estimate: a street tree adds about $8,870 to sale price and shortens time on market by 1.7 days. A separate decomposition: $7,130 to the fronting home and $5,698 in spillover to neighbors within 100 ft, totaling $12,828 per tree. Aggregated across Portland's street-tree inventory, this is ~$1.1 billion in home value and ~$45 million in annual benefits.

Why this matters for Dryad: the spillover term is the one that maps onto a Dryad parcel. The lot itself may not have a market sale comp, but every neighbor within 100 feet is captured. Plant 6 trees per lot across 9 lots = 54 trees touching ~150 to 200 nearby parcels.

### Branas et al. (2018): the cluster RCT

Philadelphia ran a true randomized controlled trial: 541 vacant lots in 110 clusters were randomly assigned to (a) full greening (grass + trees + low fence + maintenance), (b) mowing only, or (c) no intervention. After 18 months, full-greening clusters saw:

- 9.2% reduction in all crimes vs. control.
- 13.7% reduction in burglary vs. control.
- Significant reductions in residents' self-reported feelings of depression and worthlessness.

Property-value follow-on work using the same intervention found a 4.3% lift in surrounding home values in year one and a cumulative 13% lift by year six.

Why this matters for Dryad: this is the strongest causal evidence in the literature. It is an RCT, not a correlational study. The same intervention pattern — clean, plant, fence, maintain — is exactly what Dryad funds and orchestrates. A neighbor who hears "a Penn study showed this raises your home value 13%" is hearing real causal estimates, not advocacy hand-waving.

### Wachter & Gillen + Jensen & Wachter (Penn IUR): scaling Philadelphia

Susan Wachter (Wharton) and Kevin Gillen examined commercial greening, vacant-lot management, and neighborhood greening programs at scale. A pilot-scale Philadelphia LandCare effort showed adjacent-property uplift of up to 30% in one neighborhood. A subsequent modeled estimate by Shane Jensen and Wachter put the real-estate value benefit of the broader PHS LandCare program at $200,000 per intervention.

Why this matters for Dryad: PHS LandCare and Dryad are operationally similar — clean, plant, light fencing, recurring maintenance. The economics literature has already done the work of showing these specific interventions create measurable real-estate value.

### Kovacs et al. (2022): the national meta-analysis

A meta-analysis of 21 hedonic studies and 157 observations across the U.S. Key finding: the elasticity of home value with respect to neighborhood canopy is about 4x larger in neighborhoods that already have >25% tree cover than in neighborhoods at 0-10%. Off-property tree cover (canopy on the block, not on your own lot) generally drives more value than on-property cover, except at very low canopy density.

Why this matters for Dryad: Dryad lots add off-property canopy for every surrounding home. As Chadsey-Condon's overall canopy rises, the *marginal* value of each additional tree rises with it. There is a flywheel.

### Voicu & Been (NYU Furman Center): community gardens

Studied community gardens across NYC. Effects on surrounding property values were significant and largest in the poorest neighborhoods, where a high-quality community garden lifted nearby property values by up to 9.4 percentage points within five years. An ~one-third-acre garden or park within 200 to 500 feet of a home raised sale price by ~9.4%.

Why this matters for Dryad: Chadsey-Condon is a low-income neighborhood. The literature predicts the *largest* property-value effects there, not the smallest.

### Wolf (USFS, ongoing): the synthesis

Kathleen Wolf's program at the USFS has reviewed and produced primary research on urban tree economics for two decades. Across studies, the consistent headline is a ~7% premium for properties with trees over otherwise comparable properties without, with larger premiums in lower-quality neighborhoods and a 100-foot spatial spillover radius for individual street trees.

## Detroit-specific context

The Detroit-specific evidence base is thinner than Philadelphia's, but consistent:

- **Detroit Tree Equity Partnership** (city + Greening of Detroit + DTE Energy + American Forests) launched 2022 with a goal of 75,000 trees by 2027 and $30 million in neighborhood investment. Rationale documented by the partnership cites property-value uplift, energy savings, and health-equity benefits.
- **The Greening of Detroit** has planted 177,000+ trees citywide since 1989.
- **Detroit Land Bank Authority Side Lot Program** has sold 10,000+ lots to neighbors at $100. A 2025 study (Sustainable Cities and Society) on land-banking programs from 2011 to 2020 found *mixed* results across programs: the Auction program significantly increased adjacent housing values, while the Side Lot program showed positive direct effects offset by negative indirect effects, netting no significant change. **Important caveat**: Side Lot transfers ownership but does *not* fund maintenance, planting, or programming. This is exactly the gap Dryad fills.

The Detroit takeaway: simply transferring vacant lots to neighbors (Side Lot) does not by itself reliably raise values. The interventions that *do* raise values — Branas, LandCare, Voicu/Been, Donovan/Butry — all involve sustained investment in planting and maintenance. Dryad's model matches the high-evidence interventions, not the no-evidence one.

## Translating to the Dryad pitch

### The blunt arithmetic for one Dryad lot

Take a single Chadsey-Condon parcel with ~10 to 15 surrounding homes within the Donovan/Butry 100-ft spillover radius. Assume a modest version of the literature's findings:

- Each mature tree planted: ~$5,700 in spillover value to neighbors (Portland 2010 dollars; conservatively unindexed for inflation).
- 4 to 6 trees per lot: $22,800 to $34,200 in neighbor home-value uplift per Dryad parcel from trees alone.
- Add greening, fencing, and maintenance: Branas/Wachter literature predicts an additional 4% to 13% on surrounding home values, compounding over time.
- Across 9 lots: a defensible neighborhood-scale uplift of $200K to $1M+ in surrounding home value over 5 to 10 years, before counting cooling, crime, energy, stormwater, or social-capital effects.

### The selling-point framing

There are three audiences. The same evidence base supports each one with a different framing.

**For the immediate neighbor:** "The lot next to you is currently worth less than zero — it is actively dragging your home value down by 4 to 20 percent. When Dryad cleans, plants, and maintains it, the same lot starts adding 4 to 17 percent back. The Penn RCT data is the strongest causal evidence in the field."

**For the city / Detroit Land Bank:** "Side Lot transfers don't reliably raise values because they don't fund maintenance. Dryad does. Every Dryad-managed parcel converts a value-destroying liability on the tax base into a value-creating asset, with the property-value uplift accruing to surrounding homeowners and, eventually, the city's tax rolls."

**For grantmakers and donors:** "$1 in tree care returns $2 to $5 in measurable benefits. The Branas RCT shows 9.2% crime reduction. The Voicu/Been work shows the largest property-value gains land in the poorest neighborhoods. Dryad makes those numbers operational, autonomously, on parcels too small for any city program to bother with."

## Honest caveats

1. **Green gentrification is real.** The same property-value uplift that benefits existing owners can price out renters, especially in already-pressured markets. Chadsey-Condon's current ownership and rental dynamics matter. The Branas, Voicu/Been, and Wolf literatures all flag this. Dryad's model — neighborhood-controlled, lot-by-lot, paired with community programming — is structurally less prone to displacement than top-down redevelopment, but the risk is not zero.
2. **Detroit ≠ Portland or Philadelphia.** The hedonic price gradients are smaller in markets with weak baseline values. A 13 percent lift on a $40,000 home is $5,200, not $50,000. The *percentages* hold; the dollar magnitudes scale with the local market.
3. **Effects compound with maintenance, not with planting alone.** The Side Lot null result and the Branas mowing-only arm both confirm: it is the *sustained* care, not the one-time intervention, that creates value. This is exactly the variable Dryad's autonomous loop holds constant.
4. **Time horizon.** Most studies measure effects over 5 to 10 years. Year-one signal is real but smaller. The pitch should be honest about this.
