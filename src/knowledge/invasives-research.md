# Invasive Species - Extended Research & Economic Impact

## The Scale of the Problem
- Invasive species have cost $326.7 billion in urban areas globally (1965–2021), averaging $5.7 billion/year
- North America: $26 billion/year in invasive species management costs since 2010
- Invasive species cost 17× more than previously estimated and outpace costs of extreme weather attributable to climate change
- Cost hotspots are predominantly in densely populated urban areas - exactly where Dryad operates

## Tree of Heaven (Ailanthus altissima) - Present on Dryad's Parcels
- A single mature tree produces 300,000 seeds/year
- Releases allelopathic toxins (ailanthone) into soil that inhibit native plant germination - actively poisoning ground for competitors
- Thrives in disturbed urban soils; creates dense monocultures on vacant lots
- Primary host for spotted lanternfly (Lycorma delicatula)
- Grows 3–6 feet per year; resprouts aggressively from roots after cutting
- P3 priority in Dryad system: requires positive ID before removal (resembles native Staghorn Sumac)
- ID tips: crushed leaves smell like rancid peanut butter; leaflet bases have 1–3 glandular teeth; bark is smooth gray on young trees, develops diamond patterns with age

## Spotted Lanternfly (SLF) - Already in Metro Detroit
- First Michigan colony confirmed in Oakland County, 2022
- Now confirmed in Wayne, Oakland, Lenawee, and Macomb counties - all surrounding Dryad's parcels
- Spotted in Dearborn (November 2025) - less than 10 miles from Dryad's lots
- Feeds on 70+ plant species but requires Tree of Heaven for reproductive cycle
- Michigan produces 11,000 acres of juice grapes and 3,300 acres of wine grapes - all at risk
- SLF damage to grapevines includes reduced yield, vine death, and honeydew/sooty mold
- Tree of Heaven removal is the primary management strategy for SLF population control
- Michigan's Invasive Species Program surveyed 110,000+ acres in 2025, SLF as priority target
- USDA quarantine zones expanding across eastern US

## Japanese Knotweed (Reynoutria japonica) - P2 Herbaceous Invasive
- Costs £165.6 million/year in UK alone (most-studied market)
- Can devalue properties by 15% - a major factor in real estate transactions
- Rhizomes push through pavement, foundations, drainage systems causing structural damage
- Forms dense thickets that choke out all native plants and alter soil chemistry
- Root systems don't stabilize soil - increases erosion and streambank destabilization
- Extremely persistent: can regrow from a fragment as small as 0.7 grams
- Requires multi-year treatment with repeated herbicide application or complete excavation
- Present on Detroit vacant lots, especially near water features and foundations

## The Cascade Effect - Why Early Detection Matters
- Invasive monoculture → loss of native plant diversity → pollinator habitat collapse → reduced pollination → further native decline → soil degradation → increased stormwater runoff → more erosion → more habitat loss
- Each step compounds the next - intervention cost increases exponentially with delay
- A single colonized lot becomes a seed source for surrounding blocks within 2–3 growing seasons
- Early detection and rapid response (EDRR) is the most cost-effective invasive management strategy
- Dryad's iNaturalist monitoring provides exactly this: continuous surveillance through community science

## Why Dryad's Invasive Detection Approach Works
- Follows Michigan's Invasive Species Program recommendation: continuous monitoring, early detection, rapid response
- Community science volunteers serve as the sensor network - observations flow through iNaturalist API
- AI agent queries iNaturalist with GPS bounding box for Dryad parcels
- Any observation flagged as P1, P2, or P3 invasive triggers the decision loop
- Agent coordinates contractor removal, verifies completion via GPS-tagged photos
- Every removal recorded onchain as a milestone - creating auditable invasive management records
- Removes Tree of Heaven = removes SLF breeding habitat = protects Michigan agriculture
- Scale potential: same monitoring pattern can be applied to any parcel network
