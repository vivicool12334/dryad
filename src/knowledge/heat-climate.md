# Heat Islands & Climate Resilience - Research & Data

*Compiled April 2026. Investor-grade citations. Detroit-weighted.*

## Heat-Related Mortality in Detroit

### Heatwave Mortality Modeling
- Detroit would suffer **107 heat-related deaths** during a severe heatwave (with operational grid) - 15 deaths per 100,000 population
- For comparison: Atlanta = 2 deaths, Phoenix = 19 deaths in same scenario
- **Heat + blackout scenario: estimated 220 deaths** in Detroit (assumes functioning healthcare; actual likely higher)
- Higher rate than hotter cities due to limited AC access and higher poverty rates
- Source: Stone et al., PMC study "How Blackouts during Heat Waves Amplify Mortality and Morbidity Risk"

### Racial Mortality Gap
- **Black Detroiters: 5.3% higher mortality rate** during heat waves compared to white Detroiters
- Attributed primarily to lack of air conditioning access related to economic disadvantage
- Source: Cooling Detroit report (Urban Energy Justice Lab); O'Neill et al. 2005

### Vulnerable Populations
- Medicare/Medicaid beneficiaries 65+ show **10% higher heat-related ED visits** and **7% higher hospitalization rates** during heat waves
- **4% higher mortality rate** in this population during heat waves (IRR 1.04, 95% CI 1.01-1.07)
- Source: PMC study "Heat Waves and Adverse Health Events Among Dually Eligible Individuals"

## Detroit's Heat Inequality

### Exposure
- **86% of Detroit neighborhoods** experience temps 8°F+ higher than surrounding rural areas - **highest percentage of any major US city**
- Comparison: New York 78%, Houston 73%, Dallas 75%
- **3,160 residents** live in intra-urban heat islands 10°F+ hotter than nearby areas
- Detroit ranked **#2 most vulnerable large city in the U.S.** to extreme heat events (Knowlton et al. 2012)
- Source: Climate Central analysis; Michigan State University

### Tree Canopy Disparity
- Detroit overall: **24.3% tree canopy coverage** (target: 40%)
- Suburbs have **~30% more tree canopy** than Detroit's city core
- Redlined, predominantly Black neighborhoods have significantly lower canopy
- Tree canopy declined **23% since 1950**; 286 of 875 block groups below 75 on Tree Equity Score
- Source: Climate Central; Greening of Detroit; Michigan Tech study (2025)

### Root Cause
- Historical white flight (1950s-1960s) concentrated tree cover in wealthy suburbs
- 19% of remaining canopy sits over unprotected vacant land - vulnerable to development
- Only 6% of Detroit's land is parks/recreation vs. 15% national median
- Source: Urban Energy Justice Lab; Planet Detroit; Detroit Future City

## Vacant Lots as Heat Drivers

### Physical Mechanism
- **59% of Detroit's air temperature variance is related to imperviousness** (Zhang et al. 2011)
- Detroit has **~24 square miles of vacant land** = 17% of total city area
- Bare/abandoned lots can exceed ambient temperature by **15-20°F** (surface temp); air temp differences typically 2-5°F
- Impervious surfaces absorb solar energy and re-radiate as heat for hours after sunset
- Source: Urban Energy Justice Lab; SEMCOG Vulnerability Assessment

## Detroit's Climate Adaptation Plan

### What Exists
- First-ever Climate Action Plan (2017) by Detroit Climate Action Collaborative
- 2023 Climate Strategy with 4 pillars including "Prioritizing vulnerable residents & adapting to change"
- **172% increase in hot, humid days** over past 52 years; **338% increase in hot, dry days**
- Goal: **75,000 trees planted by 2027** (6,500+ planted to date)
- Source: Planet Detroit (2023); GLISA/Detroit Climate Action Plan

### What's Funded
- $1 million EPA Shoreline Cities Green Infrastructure grant (2014) for pilot projects on 40 vacant lots
- $3 million/year Detroit Water & Sewerage green infrastructure investment
- Detroit Tree Equity Partnership: 75,000 trees, $30M, 300 jobs
- Source: C2ES collaboration report; American Forests

### What's Unfunded
- Full citywide deployment to reach 40% canopy
- Comprehensive cooling center network
- Universal AC assistance program
- Plan criticized as "reactive" not proactive; uses conditional language

## Cost of Heat-Related Illness

### National
- **~235,000 ED visits and 56,000+ hospital admissions** annually from extreme heat in US
- **~$1 billion in summer healthcare costs** alone
- For every 10 additional extreme heat days (>90°F), Medicaid transfer payments increase by nearly 1% (**$11.78 per capita**)
- Source: Center for American Progress; VCU study (2023)

### Detroit
- University of Michigan Graham Institute: **$302 million annual health burden** from air pollution in tri-county Detroit area (heat-specific costs not disaggregated)
- Heat-related costs per person: **$10-40/person annually in Detroit** vs. <$1-10 in low-exposure areas
- Source: Graham Institute, UM; Catalyst Grant report (Gronlund)

## Measured Cooling Effects from Green Space

### Air Temperature Reduction
- Street-level trees: **2-4°F cooling** in direct vicinity
- Neighborhood-scale tree cover ≥40%: **4-5°C (7-9°F) cooling** maximum (Madison, WI threshold study)
- Each 1% increase in canopy cover: 0.01°C air temperature reduction
- Surface temperature under tree canopy vs. bare pavement: **15°F+ difference**
- Source: Zhang et al. (2013, Shenzhen); Knight et al. (2021) review; Nowak et al. (2006-2014)

### Detroit Projection
- To achieve 40% canopy (up from 24.3%): estimated maximum cooling = **4-5°C (7-9°F) neighborhood-wide** if distributed equitably
- Current 6,500 trees toward 75,000-tree goal: modest but measurable local cooling in targeted neighborhoods
