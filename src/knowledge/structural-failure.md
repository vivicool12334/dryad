# Why Current Approaches Fail — Structural Analysis

*Compiled April 2026. Investor-grade citations.*

## The Tree Survival Crisis

### Establishment Mortality (Years 1-5)
- **Annual mortality rate: 6.6-7.0%** median across published studies
- Range: 25th-75th percentile = 2.81-9.33% annual mortality
- First-year mortality is worst: Sacramento = 12.2% in Year 1, dropping to 3.4% by Year 5
- **Population half-life: ~50% of a planted tree cohort dead or removed by 13-18 years after planting**
- Source: Hilbert et al., 2019, USDA Forest Service review of 16 studies; Roman et al., 2016

### What Works (and Why It's Expensive)
- East Palo Alto (Canopy nonprofit): **99.4% survival over 6 years** — intensive volunteer management + experienced arborists + structured watering
- Philadelphia (University City Green): **95.4-98.4% over 6 years** — professional arborists trained volunteers + dedicated staff coordination
- Florida (2,354 trees): **93.6% alive at 2-5 years** — critical variable was on-site irrigation (97.7% with irrigation vs. 73.8% without)
- Common factors: adequate staffing, multi-year funding, detailed site assessment, post-planting care protocols, professional supervision
- Source: Eisenman et al., 2019, "Beyond the Golden Shovel"; Roman et al.

### The Investor Takeaway
- Standard programs lose ~1 in 15 trees annually in Years 1-5
- Without dedicated maintenance after Year 5, cumulative loss approaches 50% by Year 15
- Volume metrics (trees planted) mask this reality

## The Maintenance Funding Gap

### The Cost Structure Problem
- Planting costs = **13-14% of total forestry budgets** in typical municipal programs
- But planting & establishment can represent **80%+ of total lifetime maintenance costs** during the life of a tree
- Annual maintenance per tree: **$15-50/year** once established
- Grant funders fund planting; **maintenance funding is rarely included**
- Source: Kielbaso et al., 1982; McPherson et al., 1997; GreenBlue Urban analysis

### Detroit Specifically
- Detroit spends **less than majority of major US cities** on park maintenance — about **half the national median**
- Parks & Recreation must raise **nearly half of its annual capital improvement budget from outside sources** (grants, philanthropy)
- Source: ULI Americas panel report; Planet Detroit (2022)

### The Result
- Grant cycle ends (typically 1-3 years) → municipality must absorb costs with flat/shrinking budget → trees die from lack of care → program seeks new grant for "replanting" → repeat

## The Grant Cycle Death Spiral

### The Pattern
1. Grant funds tree planting initiative (Years 1-3)
2. Grant ends; city budget cannot absorb maintenance costs
3. Trees die from lack of care (6.6-7% annually, compounding)
4. Program seeks new grant for "replanting" (repeat cycle)
5. Volunteer burnout grows; staff turnover increases
6. Nonprofits spend 60+ hours/year on applications rather than field work

### The Structural Problem
- Average nonprofit volunteer retention rate: **~65%** (35% annual churn)
- Nonprofits relying heavily on project-based grants (60%+ of budget) enter the **"nonprofit starvation cycle"** — restricted funding prevents investment in core infrastructure, admin, and long-term sustainability
- Source: Bloomerang nonprofit data; Funding for Good

### Detroit Example — Greening of Detroit
- **Operational since 1989; planted 166,000+ trees**
- Revenue structure: dependent on foundation/philanthropic grants + volunteer labor
- March 2024: **$1.8 million, 3-year investment from Gilbert Family Foundation** — largest private donation in org's history
- This single major gift is necessary precisely because annual funding is inadequate
- Detroit Conservation Corps has graduated 71 cohorts but faces budgetary uncertainty annually
- Source: Greening of Detroit; Gilbert Family Foundation

### Detroit Future City Plan
- Strategic framework issued ~2016 for integrated open space network
- Explicitly states "no single funding source will support implementation"
- Limited progress — many recommendations unfunded due to municipal budget constraints

## Volunteer Fatigue

### The Burnout Data
- **62% of sustainability professionals report burnout** (Oxford Brookes survey, 159 practitioners)
- **69% find it difficult to stay motivated; only 53% feel they receive adequate well-being support**
- Since 2019, volunteers citing "too much stress" rose from **9% to 14%** (UK NCVO, 2023)
- Source: Oxford Brookes/Climate Change Coaches; NCVO Time Well Spent 2023

### Environmental Volunteer Specific
- 38.5% of lapsed volunteers cite feeling undervalued or "not part of the team"
- Time constraints and inconvenient locations = primary obstacles
- Programs without paid staff coordination typically **fail within 3-5 years**
- Organizations that succeed 10+ years employ professional arborists/landscape managers alongside (not instead of) volunteer labor
- Source: Gabard (1997); NCVO; multiple organizational studies

## What Actually Works Long-Term

### Success Factors (Eisenman et al., 2019)
- Adequate staffing with arboricultural expertise
- Multi-year (not one-time) funding commitment
- Detailed site assessment and species selection
- Post-planting care protocols (3-5 years minimum)
- Community involvement with professional supervision
- Measurement and accountability systems

### Key Insight
- **Paid staff + volunteer labor** outperforms both purely volunteer-driven AND purely top-down approaches
- The missing piece: a persistent operational entity that never stops maintaining

## The Operating Model Failure — Summary

| Dimension | Current Model | Why It Fails |
|-----------|---------------|-------------|
| **Funding** | Annual/cyclical grants | Ends; maintenance unfunded |
| **Maintenance** | Municipal budget (shrinking) | Competes with services; deferred |
| **Staff** | Nonprofit + volunteers | High turnover; burnout |
| **Metrics** | Trees planted (volume) | Ignores survival and outcomes |
| **Decision-making** | Top-down planning | Residents don't invest in outcomes |
| **Accountability** | Funder reporting | Disconnected from community needs |
| **Scalability** | One-off projects | Site-dependent, not systematic |
