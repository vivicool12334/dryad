# The Ecological Time Bomb - Invasive Species, SLF, & Unmanaged Urban Land

*Compiled April 2026. Investor-grade citations. Detroit/Michigan-weighted.*

## Spotted Lanternfly: 2025-2026 Michigan Status

### Current Distribution (November 2025)
- **6 Michigan counties confirmed:** Jackson, Lenawee, Macomb, Monroe, Oakland, Wayne
- Wayne County (Detroit metro) = **established infestation zone** - residents no longer need to report sightings (endemic status)
- Spotted in **Dearborn** (November 2025) - less than 10 miles from Dryad's lots
- Jackson County newly confirmed (2025)
- Source: MDARD, November 6, 2025 press release; MSU Extension

### No Quarantine Yet
- Michigan has NOT established state-level quarantine measures (unlike PA, OH, MD)
- MDARD is "evaluating potential spotted lanternfly quarantine measures" as of November 2025
- No restrictions on movement of vehicles, firewood, outdoor equipment from infested counties
- Source: MDARD; MSU Extension; Ask.Extension expert response (August 2025)

### Agricultural Threat to Michigan
- Michigan's agriculture and food industry generates **over $90 billion annually**
- **11,000 acres of juice grapes + 3,300 acres of wine grapes** - all at risk
- SLF feeds on **70+ plant species** including grapes, apples, hops, stone fruits, hardwoods
- Tree of Heaven (preferred host) abundant on urban vacant lots = breeding ground reservoirs
- Source: MDARD; MSU Extension; Bridge Michigan

### Pennsylvania Cost Model (Predictive for Michigan)
- Current (in quarantine zone): **$50.1 million annual damage, 484 job losses**
- Adjacent county spread: $89.2 million annual, 843 job losses
- **Statewide spread: $324.9 million annual damage, 2,810 job losses**
- Worst-case: **$554 million annually, 4,987 job losses**
- Source: Penn State College of Agricultural Sciences economic impact study, 2019-2020

### Climate Acceleration
- Without seasonal temperature differences, SLF can reproduce **multiple times per year** (vs. once annually)
- Warming winters = increased larval/nymphal survival rates
- Climate suitability modeling shows broader potential range than historically predicted
- Expansion into northern Michigan, Wisconsin, Minnesota likely within 5-10 years
- Source: Frontiers in Insect Science; U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service (2023)

## Emerald Ash Borer Aftermath in Detroit

### The Canopy Collapse
- Detroit lost **over 1 million ash trees** to emerald ash borer
- Regional loss (southeast Michigan): **30 million ash trees** since 2002 discovery
- Detroit pre-EAB had ash-dominated canopy (legacy of post-Dutch Elm Disease replanting) - near-total canopy loss in many neighborhoods
- Source: Lionel Bradford, President, Greening of Detroit (2025); MSU Forest Carbon and Climate Program

### Economic Impact
- Detroit area cost: estimated **$38 million** in treatment, removal, and replacement (2009-2019)
- Broader 25-state estimate: mean discounted cost **$10.7 billion** for 17 million ash trees
- Source: MSU Firewood Education/Forest Health database; Smith et al., *Arboriculture & Urban Forestry*

### Compounding Effects
- Ash loss created ideal conditions for Tree of Heaven and spotted lanternfly - sunny, compacted, hot urban microsites
- Summer temperatures in low-canopy neighborhoods **5-10°F higher** than nearby treed areas
- Soil exposure and compaction accelerated invasive weed colonization
- Source: SUNY ESF; MSU FCCP white paper

## Vacant Lots as Invasive Species Seed Banks

### Seed Bank Dynamics
- Upper soil layer of vacant lots: **147 seedlings per square meter**
- **43+ invasive species** dominating seed banks in urban vacant lots
- Vacant lots consistently show **70-90% non-native species composition**
- Seed banks remain viable for **3-22+ years**
- Lot age does NOT predict seed bank composition (continuous replenishment occurs)
- Source: Urban seed bank studies (Yale HIXSON lab; University of Michigan; Baltimore LTER)

### Detroit Scale
- Typical lot: 2,000-5,000 m² × 147 seeds/m² = **294,000-735,000 viable invasive seeds per lot**
- 6,000+ vacant parcels = estimated **1.76-4.41 trillion invasive seeds** in Detroit's vacant lot network
- Each colonized lot becomes a seed source for surrounding blocks within 2-3 growing seasons
- Removing canopy without addressing seed bank (>3-year drawdown) results in **70-80% reinvasion within 2 years**

## Soil Degradation on Vacant Lots

### What Happens Over 5-20 Years of Invasive Monoculture
- Heavy metal contamination (legacy industrial sites): 70-100% of Detroit vacant lots
- Compaction: pedestrian traffic creates 50%+ bulk density increase
- Organic matter depletion from disturbance (dumping, fire, trampling, vandalism)
- Nutrient depletion from invasive monocultures extracting nitrogen without replacement
- Source: EPA "Improving Urban Soils" fact sheet (2015); Anderson & Minor 2016

### Restoration Costs Escalate with Delay
- Low-input broadcast seeding: $500-1,500 per 0.1 ha ($5K-15K per lot), 40-60% success rate
- Professional contractor restoration: **$13,500 for 0.2 ha lot** (Cleveland EPA case study)
- Contaminated soil remediation: **$20K-50K+ per lot** (1-3 years before planting viable)
- Annual maintenance: $500-3,000/lot/year for 3-5 years post-planting
- Source: EPA; Minnesota/Chicago urban lot restoration studies; OSU soil science (Perry et al. 2021)

## Pollinator Crisis in SE Michigan

### Native Bee Study
- Sampled **3,300+ bees from 143 species** across 26 sites in SE Michigan
- Female bumble bee populations declined significantly with urbanization
- Detroit supported comparatively high native bee populations attributed to abundant vacant lots providing flowering plants and nesting substrate
- BUT: invasive monocultures (Tree of Heaven, phragmites) eliminate floral diversity, reducing native bee food sources
- Source: University of Michigan-led study (2019), published in PMC

### Detroit Bee Infrastructure
- Detroit Hives: **225 hives across 70+ locations** (2025)
- 5 vacant lots transformed into botanical gardens
- Coverage gap: 5 restored lots vs. 6,000 vacant = **0.08% coverage**
- Honey bee colony loss (Michigan): **30% annually** (national pattern post-2005)
- Source: Detroit Hives/Bee City; MDOT Pollinator Habitat Program

## The Cost of Doing Nothing: 10-20 Year Projection

### Global Invasive Species Cost Trends
- 1970-2017: **$1.288 trillion** total invasive species costs globally
- Annual mean: $26.8 billion/year - but 2017 alone was **$162.7 billion** (6.1x average, accelerating)
- Costs doubling every 6 years
- Source: *Nature* journal study (2023); UN-backed IPBES report

### Michigan Unmanaged Scenario (2045)
- Spotted lanternfly statewide spread: **$400-650 million annually**
- Emerald ash borer ongoing costs: **$20-50 million/year**
- Other invasives (ToH, phragmites, knotweed): **$50-100 million/year**
- Pollinator/ecosystem services collapse: **$200-400 million/year** (crop pollination value)
- Lost stormwater management from eliminated canopy: **$100-200 million/year**
- **Total estimated 2045 annual cost (unmanaged): $1.0-1.8 billion/year regionally**
- Proactive management alternative: **$100-200 million/year** - a **5-10x ROI**
- Sources: Penn State modeling; USDA Forest Service; IPBES; MSU FCCP
