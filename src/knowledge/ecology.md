# Lakeplain Oak Openings & Wet Prairie

Pre-settlement, southeastern Michigan's glacial lakeplain supported vast oak savannas and tallgrass prairies. Two MNFI community types are relevant to Dryad's parcels:

## Lakeplain Oak Opening (G2/S1 - Globally Imperiled)
- Canopy cover: 40-60% (NOT closed forest - this is a savanna maintained by fire)
- Dominant canopy: Bur oak (Quercus macrocarpa), white oak (Q. alba), swamp white oak (Q. bicolor), pin oak (Q. palustris)
- Understory: New Jersey tea, prairie willow, hazelnut
- Ground layer: Big bluestem, Indian grass, prairie dropseed, side-oats grama
- Key forbs: Purple coneflower, black-eyed Susan, wild bergamot, butterfly milkweed, rough blazing star, golden alexanders, Culver's root, wild lupine
- Historical maintenance: Low-intensity fire every 3-7 years
- Modern maintenance: Selective mowing of competing vegetation
- MNFI reference: https://mnfi.anr.msu.edu/communities/description/10710/Lakeplain-Oak-Openings

## Lakeplain Wet Prairie (G2/S1 - less than 1% survives)
- Species-rich tallgrass prairie on seasonally wet lakeplain
- Up to 200 species in a single remnant
- Cord grass, blue-joint grass, swamp milkweed, mountain mint, dense blazing star
- MNFI reference: https://mnfi.anr.msu.edu/communities/description/10672/lakeplain-wet-prairie

## Invasive Species Priority System

### P1 - Woody Invasives (contractor removal required)
- European Buckthorn (Rhamnus cathartica) - dense monocultures, alters soil nitrogen
- Glossy Buckthorn (Frangula alnus) - thrives in wet lakeplain soils
- Autumn Olive (Elaeagnus umbellata) - nitrogen fixer, outcompetes natives
- Asian Bush Honeysuckle (Lonicera maackii) - early leaf-out shades native seedlings
- Multiflora Rose (Rosa multiflora) - impenetrable thickets
- Oriental Bittersweet (Celastrus orbiculatus) - strangles canopy trees

### P2 - Herbaceous Invasives (monitor, manual removal)
- Non-native Phragmites (P. australis subsp. australis) - 15-foot monocultures. IMPORTANT: Native Phragmites (subsp. americanus) has reddish stems and grows in mixed stands - LEAVE IT.
- Reed Canary Grass (Phalaris arundinacea) - dominates wet areas
- Purple Loosestrife (Lythrum salicaria) - wetland monocultures
- Spotted Knapweed (Centaurea stoebe) - allelopathic
- Garlic Mustard (Alliaria petiolata) - disrupts mycorrhizal networks critical to oaks
- Japanese Knotweed (Reynoutria japonica) - extremely persistent, grows through asphalt

### P3 - Assessment Required
- Tree of Heaven (Ailanthus altissima) - 300K seeds/yr, ailanthone toxin. Resembles native Staghorn Sumac. Confirm ID by leaf scars and smell before removal.

## Restoration Timeline
- Year 0: Invasive removal, soil test, dormant seeding, 300 saplings (80 Bur Oak, 60 Swamp White Oak, 40 White Oak, 30 Pin Oak, 20 Hickory, 30 Redbud, 20 Serviceberry, 20 Elderberry)
- Years 1-2: Weedy growth (normal), mow 2-3x at 6" to favor prairie seedlings
- Years 2-3: Selective mowing, prairie grasses 1-3 ft, first forb blooms
- Years 3-5: Prairie grasses dominate (4-6 ft), pollinator activity spikes, spot invasive removal
- Years 5-10: Mature prairie (50-100+ species), oak saplings 8-15 ft, ecosystem self-sustaining
- Year 10+: Oak savanna structure, deep root systems (6-15 ft) sequestering carbon

## Rare Species
- Kirtland's Snake (Clonophis kirtlandii) - state threatened, known from Detroit vacant lots
- Monarch butterfly (Danaus plexippus) - federal candidate, milkweed-dependent
- Purple milkweed (Asclepias purpurascens) - state special concern
- Rusty-patched bumble bee (Bombus affinis) - federally endangered, historically present
