# Green Space–Health Pipeline - Research & Data

*Compiled April 2026. Investor-grade citations. Detroit-weighted.*

## Childhood Asthma in Detroit

### Detroit's Asthma Burden
- **14.3% physician-diagnosed + 14.3% undiagnosed** = ~28.6% of Detroit children affected
- Asthma hospitalization rates for children: **3x statewide average**
- Adult asthma rate: **16.2%** (2017-2019) vs. 11.1% statewide - **double the state average**
- Asthma-related mortality: **>2x state average**
- **Detroit rated "most challenging city in nation to live with asthma"** (AAFA, 2024-2025)
- "F" rating from American Lung Association for air quality
- Source: Michigan DHHS (2021); Community Action Against Asthma (CAAA); AAFA

### Air Quality Context
- Southwest Detroit PM2.5: **17.5 μg/m³** mean (EPA standard: <12 μg/m³)
- Mobile emissions account for ~40% of PM2.5 (UM 2023)
- Ranked **13th worst city (2024), 6th worst (2025)** for year-round particulate pollution
- On high-ozone days, students with asthma score **10% lower in math and reading**
- Source: Planet Detroit; University of Michigan School of Public Health

### Green Space Connection
- Children with asthma near tree-lined streets have **lower symptom severity**
- Schools in polluted, lower-income neighborhoods have less green space/tree cover
- Source: CAAA studies; University of Michigan SPH

## Cardiovascular Disease & Green Space

### Meta-Analysis (53 Studies, 100+ Million People)
- Every **0.1 unit increase in NDVI** (vegetation index) = **2-3% reduction in:**
  - Cardiovascular disease mortality
  - Ischemic heart disease mortality
  - Cerebrovascular disease mortality
  - Stroke incidence & prevalence
- Spans 18 countries, North America, Europe, Oceania
- Source: Gascon et al. systematic review; Cardiology Advisor

### Florida 5-Year Study (Medicare)
- **229,034 older adults** in "high greenness" areas had **9% lower incidence of new cardiovascular diseases** vs. "low greenness"
- Conditions: MI, atrial fibrillation, heart failure, ischemic heart disease, hypertension, stroke

### Specific CV Risk Factors Reduced by Green Space
- Lower salivary cortisol (stress biomarker)
- Reduced heart rate, systolic & diastolic blood pressure
- Lower type 2 diabetes incidence
- Improved HDL cholesterol
- Forest bathing (7-day exposure): significant decrease in systolic BP in hypertensive patients (Wu et al. 2020)

### Walkability + Nature Combined
- AHA 2023: High nature + high walkability = **9% lower odds of CV risk factors, 4% lower odds of CVD**
- Green space alone without walkability showed counterintuitive higher CVD risk (neighborhood selection confound)

## Mental Health (Beyond Philadelphia)

### The Benchmark - Philadelphia RCT
- 540 vacant lots greened; cost $1,000-3,000 per lot
- 18-month follow-up:
  - **40% reduction in feeling depressed**
  - **50% reduction in feeling worthless**
  - Below poverty line: **68.7% reduction in depression** (p = 0.007)
- Trash cleanup alone = NO mental health benefit; greening was the active ingredient
- Source: South, Kondo, Branas et al., *JAMA Network Open*, 2018

### Dose-Response Studies
- **≥120 minutes/week in nature** = significantly associated with good health & wellbeing (White et al., *Scientific Reports*)
- **30 minutes/week** in green space reduces depression prevalence by **up to 7%** and high blood pressure by **9%** (Shanahan et al.)
- Mental health benefits observed at lower doses in high-greenness areas

### Broader Evidence
- Depression: Reduced odds with green space exposure; stronger for perceived quality than access alone
- Anxiety: Lower levels with higher green space quality and usage
- Mood disorders, substance use, suicide-related outcomes: Protective association in youth populations
- Swedish longitudinal study: Women gaining access to "serene" green space showed significant mental health improvement
- Social cohesion appears to *mediate* the green space–mental health effect

## Life Expectancy in Detroit

### The Disparity
- **Overall Detroit:** 69-72 years (below Michigan avg 78 & US avg 76)
- **By race:** Black Detroiters 68 years; White Detroiters 76 years (**8-year gap**)
- **Most extreme gap:** Midtown (69 years) vs. Grosse Pointe (82 years) = **13-year difference within 10 miles**
- **Lowest recorded:** 62-67 years in some East Detroit census tracts (near industrial areas with high pollution + low green space)
- **Highest:** 85-87 years in affluent suburbs with substantial tree cover

### Green Space & Longevity (Detroit-Specific)
- **Detroit Neighborhood Health Study (157 participants):** Presence of green space and large trees = **associated with lower mortality risk** based on epigenetic markers
- Abandoned cars, poor road conditions, nonart graffiti = higher mortality risk
- Association stronger in women
- Redlined neighborhoods (1930s lending maps) **directly correlate** with current low life expectancy areas

### Access Gap
- Detroit Future City goal: 95% of residents within 10-min walk (½ mile) of park
- Current: **87% of Detroit buildings >¼ mile from any green space; 53% >½ mile away**
- Source: Trust for Public Land; Michigan Tech study (2025)

## Air Quality from Urban Vegetation

### National Scale (US Forest Service)
- **17.4 million tons of air pollution** removed annually by US trees/forests
- Health value: **$6.8 billion** (range $1.5-13B)
- **Avoided mortality: >850 deaths/year**
- Prevented acute respiratory symptoms: **670,000 incidences/year**
- Most removal in rural areas but **highest health impact in urban areas** (population density)
- Source: USDA Forest Service (2010 baseline)

### Measured Pollutant Removal (Urban Trees)
- Typical air quality improvement: **0.05-0.24%** across cities
- With 100% tree cover (contiguous forest): up to **2% improvement** for PM
- Atlanta: **71.1 tons PM2.5** removed annually
- Portland, OR urban trees: NO₂ reduction valued at **$7 million/year** in healthcare savings
- **Complete canopy removes: 15% of ozone, 14% SO₂, 8% NO₂**
- Source: i-Tree Eco models; Lovett et al.; Portland air quality study

## Physical Activity & Green Space

### Key Evidence
- Parks and green spaces reliably increase physical activity, walking, cycling
- Walking/cycling in green space produces additional stress reduction beyond the exercise itself
- Australian cohort: Amount of green space predicts physical activity levels
- Philadelphia: Not having a park AND not feeling comfortable visiting = **both independently associated with higher HBP odds**

### The Safety Caveat
- Actual physical activity depends on whether people feel safe using green spaces (crime, lighting, maintenance)
- This is exactly where sustained stewardship matters - people use spaces that look cared for
