# Civic Identity & Community Ownership - Research & Data

*Compiled April 2026. Investor-grade citations. Detroit-weighted.*

## Broken Windows → Place-Based Evidence

### The Core Mechanism
- Vacant/abandoned spaces provide physical cover for firearm storage and illegal activity
- Clearing/greening removes ecological opportunity for crime
- Visible care signals deterrence to potential offenders
- Source: Lanfear, CC, "Broken Windows, Informal Social Control, and Crime," *Crime & Delinquency*, 2019

### Philadelphia Evidence (RCTs)
- **Vacant lot greening:** $1 invested → **$26 in net safety/health benefits** (up to $333 in full social value)
- **Gun violence:** 4.5% reduction in firearm assaults sustained for 45.8 months post-intervention
- **Abandoned buildings:** 13.12% reduction in gun assaults from full remediation
- Less than 5% of remediated lots became developed - no gentrification displacement
- Source: Branas et al., 2018 PNAS; Branas et al., 2021 JAMA Internal Medicine; Penn IUR

### Social Infrastructure (Eric Klinenberg)
- Vacant properties undermine "social infrastructure" - the physical places that enable informal social control
- When neighborhoods are visibly abandoned, residents lose collective efficacy and withdraw from public space
- Restoration signals the area is cared for and worth investing in
- Source: Klinenberg, *Palaces for the People* (2018)

## Collective Efficacy

### Definition (Sampson et al., 1997)
- **Collective efficacy = social cohesion + shared expectations for informal social control**
- Neighborhoods with high collective efficacy: lower crime, higher civic engagement, stronger social bonds
- Source: Sampson et al., "Neighborhoods and Violent Crime," *Science*, 1997

### What Drives It
- Frequency of casual, unstructured social interaction (parks, community events, shared projects)
- Visible signs of community care (maintained public spaces, gardens, organized cleanups)
- Resident participation in decision-making about neighborhood improvement (not decisions made FOR them)

### Measurable Outcomes
- After lot remediation: residents report increased willingness to spend time outdoors, engage neighbors, "care for this place"
- Source: Philadelphia Housing Trust Foundation study, 2018-2020

## Green Space & Social Cohesion

### The Evidence
- Routine use of local green spaces **increases social cohesion** by facilitating fleeting and meaningful encounters
- Frequency of social interaction in public space is the **strongest predictor of social cohesion** - stronger than any single amenity
- Trust and mutual respect develop through regular, unstructured interaction
- Source: Wan et al., 2021 (systematic review); Sampson & Raudenbush, 1999

### Green Space Satisfaction → Neighborhood Satisfaction
- Strong link between satisfaction with local parks and overall neighborhood satisfaction
- Depressed neighborhoods benefit most from green space access - up to **11x less green space in majority-BIPOC wards**
- Source: UK Design Council report, 2023

## Civic Identity & Stewardship

### Identity-Driven Participation
- Environmental knowledge (not self-identification as "environmentalist") predicts willingness to participate in stewardship
- **"I see myself as someone who cares for my community" → drives engagement**
- Demographic variables (race, gender, education, income) are **NOT significant predictors** of stewardship willingness
- Source: Arboriculture & Urban Forestry, 2017 (Philadelphia stewardship study)

### Community Ownership Model
- When residents experience improvements AND have decision-making power AND see direct benefit, health outcomes improve
- Political empowerment: residents who participate report higher civic engagement + local election participation
- Social cohesion: shared decision-making strengthens bonds and trust in institutions
- Source: Urban Institute, 2025; MIT Healthy Neighborhoods Study

## Detroit's Community Land History

### Historical Roots
- **1893:** Mayor Hazen Pingree's potato patch program - offered unemployed workers land + seeds during recession. Foundation of urban gardening culture.
- **1970s:** Mayor Coleman Young's Farm-A-Lot program - 7,000-10,000 participants using vacant lots for food production
- **1990s-present:** James & Grace Lee Boggs Center, Detroit Collaborative Design Center, grassroots organizations revived urban agriculture post-industrial decline

### Current Scale
- **~2,200 community gardens and farms** (2023)
- **~20,000 Detroiters engaged** in urban agriculture programs
- **D-Town Farm** (Detroit Black Community Food Security Network): Largest garden, 13+ years operation, focus on food sovereignty
- **Michigan Urban Farming Initiative:** Designated agrihood, corporate partnerships (Target, BASF, GM), workforce development
- Source: Planet Detroit (2023); Detroit Black Community Food Security Network

### Community-Driven Governance
- **Detroit Resilience Fund** uses participatory grantmaking model - first of its kind in Detroit - funding grassroots orgs led by BIPOC with least access to power
- **Keep Growing Detroit / Growing Together:** Programs combine food access + youth employment + neighborhood beautification + community decision-making
- Malik Yakini (DBCFSN): "Our purpose is to get Black people working on their own behalf"

## Voting & Political Participation

### Housing Stability → Voter Turnout
- 41% of eligible voters in homes <1 year vote; **68% of voters in homes >5 years vote**
- Implication: residential stability + community belonging drive political participation
- Source: Census 2022; David Gay, NYU Law

### Detroit Civic Engagement Baseline
- **Only 22% voter turnout** in most recent municipal election
- Participatory budgeting untested at scale in Detroit but recommended as tool to increase engagement
- Community-based nonprofits reach under-represented voters more effectively than traditional campaigns
- Source: Nonprofit VOTE, 2025; Seattle PI

## The Civic Case for Dryad

### What Investors Should Understand
1. **Physical space shapes civic identity.** Vacant lots signal abandonment and withdrawal; maintained spaces signal community worth investing in.
2. **Demographics don't predict participation.** Willingness to steward is driven by connection to community, not income/education/race.
3. **Detroit has the culture.** 2,200+ gardens, 20,000+ urban ag participants, 130+ years of community land stewardship history.
4. **The gap is operational persistence, not community will.** Detroit's community land movements are strong but fatigue-prone and grant-dependent.
5. **Dryad fills the maintenance gap.** Autonomous agent ensures lots never revert while community science engagement (iNaturalist) maintains resident connection and ownership.
